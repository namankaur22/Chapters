The Best Internship Ever

2024-25 10 min read learning

Another chapter that quietly shaped my second year was Red Bull. When I first became a Student Marketeer, I saw it as just another internship. Looking back now, I think it became one of the first places where I truly started believing in my own abilities. The best part was that I wasn't doing it alone. Swetha was already a Student Marketeer, so stepping into an entirely new environment didn't feel as intimidating. Having someone I trusted made it easier to take that first step.

I reported to Apeksha, who, to this day, remains one of the coolest managers I've ever had. Like almost every other place I found myself in, I was the youngest person on the team—the team's unofficial child. I expected that to mean I'd mostly observe while everyone else made decisions.

Instead, something completely different happened. Every idea I shared was heard. Every opinion I had was considered. Every meeting made me feel like I belonged there, despite being the youngest in the room. It was the first professional environment where I realized that respect isn't always earned through age or experience. Sometimes, people choose to give it to you first, and it's your responsibility to grow into it.

Red Bull constantly found new ways to throw me into situations I would've never willingly put myself in. Sampling missions meant walking up to complete strangers—business owners, vendors, café managers—and starting conversations I would've avoided just a year earlier. Every interaction chipped away at the fear I'd carried for so long. Slowly, talking to strangers stopped feeling terrifying and started feeling natural.

Then came my first event. The Red Bull Basement Activation. It was the first project that was entirely mine. From planning every tiny detail to coordinating people, managing timelines, solving last-minute problems and finally watching it all come together—I was responsible for every piece of it. I remember constantly wondering if I had forgotten something. I kept expecting something to go wrong. But it didn't. The event was a success. After everything was over, Apeksha appreciated me in front of the entire team. It was such a small moment. A few words. A few seconds. But I don't think she ever realized how much they stayed with me.

For someone who constantly questioned whether they were good enough, hearing someone I admired say, "You did well," meant more than I could ever explain. I carried those words into every event after that. They made me want to work harder—not because I wanted praise again, but because for the first time, I had proof that I was capable.

By the time Sphinx arrived, my two worlds had completely collided. I wasn't just organizing one of the biggest festivals in college anymore. I was also responsible for making sure Red Bull's presence on campus ran smoothly. For three days, I lived between two identities. One moment I was planning product sampling routes and deciding which events would have the highest engagement. The next, I was setting up the Red Bull sales point, coordinating with different teams, solving logistical issues, running to an event that needed me, and somehow still making it back in time for another meeting.

The Auto Expo became one of those moments I'll probably never forget. The event was being organized in collaboration with Red Bull, and just hours before it was supposed to begin, it looked like everything was falling apart. Many of the participating car owners had already left Jaipur, and for a while it genuinely felt like months of work had gone to waste. But instead of panicking, we adapted. We made calls. Found alternatives. Improvised every step of the way. And somehow, the event still happened. Maybe not exactly the way we had imagined it.But it happened. Looking back now, I think that's when I discovered something about myself that I hadn't noticed before. Pressure doesn't make me freeze.It sharpens me.

The busier life became, the calmer I somehow managed to stay. The more things demanded my attention, the better I became at finding solutions instead of excuses. That realization has stayed with me long after those three days ended. But growth has a cost. For months, my life revolved around Sphinx, Red Bull and academics.

Every day was packed. Every evening became another meeting. Every night ended with another document, another presentation or another phone call. I was constantly chasing the next deadline without stopping long enough to ask myself if I was okay.

Eventually, the exhaustion caught up with me. In February 2025, I made one of the hardest decisions of my second year. I resigned from Red Bull. Walking away wasn't easy. It wasn't because I had stopped loving the work. It was because somewhere along the way, I had forgotten that constantly giving every part of yourself to everything around you eventually leaves very little for yourself.

Leaving felt like closing a chapter that had changed me forever. Red Bull didn't just teach me how to market a product or manage an event. It taught me how to communicate with confidence. How to take ownership.How to trust my instincts. How to stay calm when everything around me felt like it was falling apart. Most importantly, it taught me that I was capable of far more than I had ever given myself credit for.

Sometimes I think internships are remembered for the companies they're with.I remember mine for the person it helped me become.
