The year I stopped being afraid of the backend

March 2023 5 min read backend, internship, node.js

For most of Year 1 and Year 2, I thought of myself as a "frontend person." Meaning: I knew HTML, CSS, a reasonable amount of JavaScript, and I could make things that looked good in a browser. The backend — servers, databases, APIs — felt like a different world. A more serious, more complicated world that I was not sure I belonged in.

Year 3 ended that story fairly abruptly.

"APIs felt like magic until I built one. Then they felt like plumbing — useful, unglamorous, completely essential."

The internship that changed how I think

Semester 5 brought my first internship — a three-month stint at a small startup building internal tools. I was hired as a frontend intern. Within two weeks, I was touching the backend.

This wasn't negligence on their part. It was the natural reality of a small team: when there are four engineers and a deadline, everyone fills gaps. The backend engineer handed me a Node.js codebase, pointed me to the route I needed to modify, and said "it's just JavaScript, you'll be fine."

He was right. And also very wrong.

It was JavaScript, yes — but JavaScript running on a server, with no DOM, no browser, no console you could inspect with your eyes. The mental model I had built for two years was designed around the browser. Suddenly I was somewhere without a browser, and everything felt upside down.

What the backend actually is

This is what I wish someone had explained to me before I started:

  • A server is just a program that listens for requests and sends back responses. That's it. The mystique is mostly marketing.
  • An API is a contract. You agree: if you send me this, I'll send you that. Express.js in Node makes writing these contracts straightforward.
  • A database is where you put things you need to remember. SQL databases organise that memory into tables. The query language (SQL) is its own thing to learn, but the concept is simple.
  • Authentication is just the backend being suspicious. It checks who you are before deciding what it'll share with you.

The first time I wrote an API route, tested it with Postman, got a 200 OK back, and saw my data come through as JSON — I felt the same thing I felt when my first webpage rendered in the browser. Something real happened because of something I wrote. That feeling doesn't get old.

Databases — the thing nobody tells you is genuinely interesting

I expected databases to be boring. Turns out database design is one of the more intellectually interesting parts of software engineering. When you're designing a schema, you're making decisions that every other layer of your app has to live with. Get it wrong and you're paying that tax forever.

The questions that actually matter in database design:

  1. What are the entities in this system, and what are their relationships?
  2. What queries will be most common, and is the schema optimised for them?
  3. What should be normalised, and what's okay to denormalise for performance?

I didn't have good answers to any of these in Year 3. But I learned to ask the questions, and asking the right questions is usually more valuable than knowing the answers.

DSA — why it matters and it's not for interviews

Semester 6 was when Data Structures and Algorithms stopped being a subject I was studying for marks and became something I actually understood why we needed.

The moment was practical. I was writing a feature at the internship — a search function over a list of several thousand records. My first implementation was a simple loop: check every record, return matches. It worked fine for small data. At scale, it was visibly slow.

I went back to the DSA concepts I'd been half-asleep through earlier. Hash maps. Binary search. The idea that the data structure you choose is a performance decision baked into your code. I refactored the search. The difference was immediate and obvious.

Algorithms aren't interview tricks. They're the vocabulary for having conversations about tradeoffs.

The code review that was hard to hear

Late in the internship, a senior engineer reviewed a PR I had opened. She didn't say it was bad. She asked questions instead — "what happens here if the network request fails?", "why did you choose a for-loop instead of a reduce here?", "if this scales to ten times the data, does this query still make sense?"

I had answers to almost none of them. I had written code that worked, but I hadn't thought about code that was resilient, readable, or efficient. Those are three different things and they all matter.

That code review was uncomfortable. It was also the most useful feedback I got all year. Being told your code works is nice. Being asked to defend your decisions is how you actually improve.

Year 3 is when I stopped thinking of myself as a frontend developer who was scared of the backend and started thinking of myself as a developer. The frontend/backend distinction matters for job descriptions. It matters much less when you're actually building something.

Year 4 is when I tried to put everything together — and built this website to prove it. That post is next.
