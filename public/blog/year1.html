The Ones Who Stayed.

2023-24 6 min read reflection

All my life, I had been the youngest in every room — pampered, protected, and loved by everyone around me. And then, just like that, seventeen years of being the family's most spoiled child came to an abrupt end.

A new country, a new city, new faces, and nothing familiar to hold on to. There was a strange feeling sitting in my chest that I still can't fully explain.

On my first night alone in the hostel, staring at the ceiling, something just didn't feel right. and that's when the reality of it all hit me. I had no idea what lay ahead. Every unfamiliar face and place suddenly felt intimidating. Within minutes, the excitement of a fresh start was swallowed by fear. A knot tightened in my stomach, my throat felt heavier with every passing second, and for the first time in my life, I felt completely on my own.

Barely five minutes into that spiral of emotions, three girls walked into my life—girls I had no idea would become my support system, my closest friends, and, most importantly, my family for the next four years. Aachal Laddha, Swarali Kuralkar, and Vedika Malpani started out as my flatmates, but somewhere along the way, they became home. I still remember our very first night together - singing at the top of our lungs, dancing around the room until midnight, and laughing over absolutely nothing. We were like teenagers at their first sleepover — because, in a way, it actually was.

Classes, clubs, societies, and new friend groups were all the hype in first year. Everyone was busy trying to find where they fit in. I was no different. To be honest, I spent so much time trying to find my place in other people’s lives that somewhere along the way, I lost myself. Or maybe I was trying so hard to become someone everyone liked that I forgot to figure out who I actually was. There were days when I was surrounded by people, laughing, joking, and making memories I’ll cherish forever, and there were also days when, despite being around so many people, I felt completely alone. Somehow, the noise around me only made the silence within me louder.

On days like those, I'd find myself walking into Aachal's room with a packet of Blue Lays in hand—her favorite chips. I'd flop onto her bed and talk about the most random things, anything to distract myself from the loneliness. I think she caught on pretty quickly. And still, she never left. What started as me trying to escape my thoughts slowly turned into sharing how I actually felt, and before I knew it, I didn't feel quite so alone anymore. Every time that loneliness found its way back, I knew she'd be there. She may not even realize it, but whenever life felt a little off, she was always the first person to make it feel right again. Maybe that's when I realized she was more than just my flatmate. She had quietly become one of my safest places.

Swarali and Vedika never missed a chance to show up for me either. Whether it was bringing me ice cream on their way back to the hostel because they knew I was upset or making sure I didn't sleep through class, they were always there in the little ways that mattered most. Every conversation that started in Aachal's room somehow ended up pulling Swarali and Vedika into it too. They never failed to make me laugh, even on my worst days. And they definitely never missed an opportunity to make fun of me either. Maybe that's exactly why it all felt so genuine.

While Aachal, Swarali, and Vedika became my family in the hostel, Dhruv Maheshwari became my best friend outside that little circle. He was my biggest support throughout the year. We'd spend entire days together in class, and most evenings were dedicated to satisfying my endless food cravings. From constantly picking on me and finding new ways to irritate me to standing up for me in rooms I wasn't even in, he'd done it all.

I still remember our first-year winter break. I couldn't go back home to Dubai, and he could. I was upset, miserable, and, if I'm being completely honest, a little jealous. He got to go home, and I didn't. But somehow, even from miles away, he didn't forget about me. When he came back, he showed up with a bag full of my favourite snacks. Somewhere along the way, I think he figured out that food was the quickest way to make me feel better. It wasn't really about the snacks, though. It was the fact that he remembered. It was little things like that that made me realize I never had to explain myself to him. Somehow, he always just knew.

It was one of the most honest friendships I've ever had. He would occasionally get frustrated by how immature I was, but he never stopped showing up. We fought all the time, but somehow, at the end of every argument, we always found our way back to each other's side. Against all odds, we were always each other's support system.

Dhruv, Aachal, Swarali, and Vedika—the four people who shaped my first year. Throughout the year, they taught me how to be a better version of myself, how to be a better friend, and how to show up for the people you care about, no matter what. They became my family, my home away from home. I don't know if they realize it, but one of the things I'm most grateful for is finding friends like them. In a year that felt so unfamiliar at times, they became the people who made it feel like home.

And maybe that was the most important thing I learned in my first year: no one is meant to do life alone. The people who stay, who choose you even on your worst days, have a way of making everything feel lighter. I may have left behind the comfort of being pampered at home, but I found a different kind of comfort in this little world of mine. Somewhere between hostel rooms, late-night conversations, shared meals, random fights, and endless laughter, four people became my family.

And when I look back at my first year, that's what I'll remember most—not the classes, not the clubs, not the achievements. I'll remember the people who made a place that felt unfamiliar become home.
