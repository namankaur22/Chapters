How I went from copying tutorials to building real things

August 2022 6 min read learning, javascript

For the first few months of Year 2, I was a tutorial addict. I'd find a YouTube series — "Learn React in 10 Hours" or "Build 5 JavaScript Projects" — start it with genuine enthusiasm, follow along step by step, and end up with something that worked. Then I'd close the laptop feeling like I'd learned something. I had not.

The brutal truth about tutorials is that following them feels like swimming, but the moment you step away from the video, you realise you were just floating. The instructor was doing the swimming. You were holding on.

"The turning point wasn't a course. It was a bug I couldn't fix for three days."

JavaScript — the language that humbled me

I came into Year 2 feeling reasonably okay about HTML and CSS. Then Semester 3 introduced JavaScript and reset everything. JavaScript is not like C++ and it is not like HTML. It has its own logic, its own way of being weird, and its own particular way of failing silently and leaving you with absolutely no idea what went wrong.

The first JavaScript thing that broke me was this. What does this refer to? Depends on where you are, how the function was called, whether you're in strict mode, and apparently the phase of the moon. I spent an embarrassing amount of time on this before I gave up trying to fully understand it and just learned enough to not shoot myself in the foot.

What I did learn properly, and what changed how I thought:

  • The DOM — that the HTML on your screen is actually a live tree of objects JavaScript can reach into and change. This was a revelation.
  • Events — click, scroll, keydown. The idea that you can say "when X happens, do Y" unlocked everything interactive.
  • Async — fetch, promises, async/await. I did not understand this in Semester 3. I understood it later. More on that below.

The hackathon — and why losing taught me more than winning would have

Semester 4. A senior from our college's tech club announced a 24-hour hackathon. The theme was "tools for students." My team of three spent the first four hours arguing about what to build, the next two hours making a Figma mockup, and then the remaining eighteen hours actually building it in a state of caffeinated panic.

We built a timetable app that let you drag-and-drop your schedule and export it to a PDF. It was buggy. The PDF export didn't work on any browser except Chrome. We couldn't get the drag-and-drop smooth in time. We presented anyway.

We didn't place. But here is what I got from that 24 hours that no tutorial ever gave me:

I made decisions under pressure. Real decisions — not "which tutorial should I watch next" but "we have 4 hours left, do we fix the export bug or polish the UI?" I chose wrong, by the way. But I chose, and I learned why it was wrong, and I'll choose differently next time.

The bug that took three days

This is the moment I tell people about when they ask when things clicked for me. It was a personal project — a habit tracker I was building to actually use myself, not to put in a portfolio. It had a streak counter that was supposed to reset to zero if you missed a day.

The streak counter would not reset. I checked the logic five times. It looked correct. I Googled the error message. Nothing useful. I asked a friend — he looked at it for twenty minutes and shrugged. I walked away from it for a full day.

On the third day, I sat down and instead of looking at the code, I described the problem out loud to nobody, like I was explaining it to someone who had never seen code. And I heard myself say: "It checks if the current date is different from the last logged date." And then I heard myself realise: I was storing the date as a string, and comparing two strings, and the format had a timezone offset that was making today's date look like yesterday.

I fixed it in four minutes. The bug had taken three days to find and four minutes to fix.

That ratio — the long, frustrating search followed by the embarrassingly simple solution — is just what debugging is. You can't shortcut the search. You just have to get comfortable sitting inside a problem until it opens up.

React — and the moment I stopped hating it

I hated React for the first month. JSX looked wrong. The component model felt like extra steps for things I could do with plain HTML. Why was everything a function? Why did I need to useState to change a number?

Then I built something with enough moving parts that plain JavaScript started getting ugly — a project dashboard where clicking a card expanded it, filtered a list, and updated a counter all at once. With vanilla JS I was manually querying the DOM in fifteen different places. With React, I had one piece of state and the UI just... reflected it.

The moment you feel React's model click is specific and real. Everything before that moment, React feels like overhead. Everything after, going back feels like giving up running water.

What changed between Year 1 and Year 2

  1. I started projects I had no idea how to finish. And then I figured them out. This is the only way.
  2. I got comfortable with the browser DevTools. The console, the network tab, the element inspector — these are not debugging tools. They are your second pair of eyes on everything.
  3. I stopped watching tutorials and started reading documentation. Docs are harder to follow but they give you the actual mental model, not just the steps.
  4. I learned to describe problems in words before trying to solve them in code. Rubber duck debugging is real and it works and I am not embarrassed about it.

Year 2 was the year I went from someone who could follow instructions to someone who could figure things out. That's not a small distinction. That's the whole thing.

Year 3 is where it got properly serious — but that involves a backend, an internship, and a very humbling codebase review. Next post.
