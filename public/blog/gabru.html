Somethings are never lost.

April 2024 7 min read home.

As if my first year hadn't already stretched every emotion I knew I was capable of feeling, life decided it wasn't done with me yet.

My parents told me they were leaving Dubai and moving back to Punjab.

I remember just sitting there, trying to process what they had said. It wasn't bad news, but it felt like loss. A quiet, unexpected kind of loss that settled in my chest before I could even understand why.

For ten years, Dubai had been more than just the city I lived in—it had become a part of me. It was where I grew up. Where every birthday, every celebration, every ordinary evening existed as a memory. It was where my closest friends lived, where my favorite cafés knew my order, where the roads felt familiar enough to call home.

It was my comfort. My peace. My constant.

Punjab wasn't foreign to me, but it wasn't *my* home either. My parents would be there, yes, but everything else that had made home feel like home would suddenly disappear.

For the second time in what felt like a year full of goodbyes, I was preparing to lose another version of my life.

What hurt the most wasn't even that they were moving.

It was that I never got the chance to say goodbye.

College had consumed every bit of my time, and before I knew it, my parents had packed up ten years of memories and left. I never got to walk through my house one last time. I never got one last shawarma with my friends or one last plate of my favorite biryani. I never got to drive down the roads that had watched me grow up. I never got to sit by the places that had quietly become my safe spaces.

I never got a proper ending.

One day, Dubai was home. The next day, it existed only in photographs, old chats, and memories.

There's something incredibly painful about not knowing that the last time is actually the last time. If someone had told me, "This is the last meal you'll have here," I would've stayed a little longer. If someone had said, "This is the last time you'll lock this door," I would've looked around one more time.

Instead, life simply moved on without asking whether I was ready.

Even now, years later, little things catch me off guard. A plate of biryani instantly transports me back to my favorite restaurant in Dubai. Every time something exciting happens, there's still a tiny part of me that wishes I could run downstairs to my best friend's apartment, barge into her room unannounced, and tell her every detail before the excitement faded.

Distance has a strange way of teaching you just how precious ordinary moments really were.But life has a funny habit of giving something back whenever it quietly takes something away.

My parents moving back to India meant that I could finally see them far more often than I ever could when they lived thousands of kilometers away. Weekend visits slowly replaced yearly vacations. Phone calls became hugs. Celebrations no longer had to happen through a screen.

My mother may have had to build another house from scratch, but somewhere along the way, I realized something she had probably known all along. Home was never the walls.Home was wherever she was.

Over the next few academic years, Punjab slowly stopped feeling like a place I was visiting and started feeling like somewhere I belonged. I still found myself craving the shawarmas from Dubai, but I also found comfort in hot plates of chole bhature. The unfamiliar slowly became familiar.

The only thing that never really got easier was missing my friends. I think my parents saw that. They saw the loneliness I tried so hard to hide. They knew there was still a part of me grieving a life I had left behind.

And like parents somehow always do, they found a way to heal a piece of me they couldn't fix with words. That's when Gabru walked into our lives. My first pet. My best friend.

I don't think he will ever understand how much he changed my life. From the moment his tiny paws stepped into our home, the house felt fuller somehow. Quieter days became happier. Empty rooms suddenly had someone running through them. Every visit back home now had a countdown attached to it—not because of a place, but because I couldn't wait to wrap my arms around him again.

Every video call with my dad somehow turns into a Gabru update. Every conversation includes stories about whatever new mischief he's created. Every trip home begins with wondering whether he'll recognize me and ends with him refusing to leave my side.

He proudly shows off the same five tricks every single time, and somehow, I clap just as enthusiastically each time. Because it isn't really about the tricks. It's about him.

Somewhere between the wagging tail that greeted me at the door, the countless cuddles, and the silent companionship, I stopped feeling so alone.

Gabru never cared whether I was having the best day of my life or the worst. He never asked why I was crying. He never questioned my silences. He simply sat beside me as if to remind me that I didn't have to carry everything by myself.

He loved me when I laughed. He loved me when I cried. He loved me when I was home.And somehow, he loved me just as fiercely when I was hundreds of kilometers away, waiting for my next visit.

For the longest time, I believed that leaving Dubai meant leaving home behind.Now I know I was wrong. Home was never just a city. It was the people who held me together when everything around me was changing. It was my mother's embrace after months apart. It was my father's voice on the other end of a video call. It was friends who still felt close despite the distance. And sometimes, home looked like a golden dog waiting impatiently at the gate, wagging his tail as though I'd only been gone for five minutes instead of five months.

Maybe that's the beautiful thing about home. You don't lose it. It simply finds new ways to love you.
